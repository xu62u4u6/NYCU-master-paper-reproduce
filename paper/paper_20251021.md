摘要

肺腺鱗狀細胞癌是一種罕見且具侵襲性的非小細胞肺癌亞型，在組織學上定義為腺癌與鱗狀細胞癌成分並存。其雙相結構常使診斷變得複雜，且目前尚無有效的標靶治療方法。雖然肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌的基因體特徵已被廣泛解析，但對於肺腺鱗狀細胞癌的基因體特徵仍所知有限。本研究旨在從亞型層級與成分層級描繪肺腺鱗狀細胞癌的基因體圖譜。我們對二十九位肺腺鱗狀細胞癌患者進行全外顯子定序，其中十一例同時具有配對的額外腺癌與鱗狀細胞癌成分，並使用本實驗室自建流程處理。此外，亦收集並重新處理公開可得的肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌全外顯子定序資料作為比較。為促進後續的整合性分析與視覺化，我們開發了以 Python 實作的工具套件 pymaftools。本研究涵蓋了單核苷酸變異及小片段插入與缺失的分析、基因體指標分析以及拷貝數變異分析，並結合分群分析、相似度評估、降維方法以及機器學習分類模型。在亞型層級，肺腺鱗狀細胞癌展現出介於肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌之間的突變與基因體特徵，包括 TP53 與 EGFR 突變率、腫瘤突變負荷以及與吸菸相關的變異模式。拷貝數變異分析揭示了顯著差異存在於三號染色體長臂，而相似度分析與降維方法則一致地將肺腺鱗狀細胞癌定位於接近肺腺癌但仍介於肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌之間的位置。在成分層級，腺癌與鱗狀細胞癌成分共享大多數驅動基因突變，並呈現大致一致的拷貝數變異輪廓，且克隆結構分析支持單株起源。特徵分群突顯了三號染色體長臂共擴增的重要性，特別是在區分成分層級差異方面。此外，以肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌訓練的機器學習分類器，透過機率型軟分數將肺腺鱗狀細胞癌樣本置於兩者之間的中間位置。整體而言，這些發現表明肺腺鱗狀細胞癌是一種介於肺腺癌與肺鱗狀細胞癌之間的過渡性分子狀態，支持單株起源假說，並受到三號染色體長臂擴增事件的影響。本研究建立了肺腺鱗狀細胞癌的基因體框架，提供了可望改善診斷策略並推動未來治療開發的基礎。

Abstract

Adenosquamous carcinoma (ASC) of the lung is a rare and aggressive non-small cell lung cancer subtype, histologically defined by the coexistence of adenocarcinoma (AC) and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) components. Its biphasic architecture often complicates diagnosis, and no effective targeted therapies are currently available. Although the genomic features of lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) and lung squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC) have been extensively characterized, those of ASC remain poorly understood. In this study, we delineated the genomic landscape of ASC at both the subtype and component levels. Whole-exome sequencing (WES) was performed on 29 ASC patients, including 11 with matched AC and SCC components, and processed with our in-house pipeline. Publicly available LUAD and LUSC WES cohorts were reprocessed with the same pipeline for comparative analyses. To facilitate integrative downstream analyses and visualizations, we developed a Python-based toolkit, pymaftools. Our analyses encompassed single-nucleotide variants, small insertions/deletions, genomic metrics, and copy-number variations (CNVs), complemented by clustering, similarity measures, dimensionality reduction, and machine learning–based classification. At the subtype level, ASC displayed mutational and genomic features intermediate between LUAD and LUSC, including EGFR and TP53 mutation rates, tumor mutational burden, and smoking-associated patterns. CNV profiling revealed significant differences at chromosome 3q, while similarity and dimensionality-reduction analyses consistently placed ASC closer to LUAD but occupying an intermediate position along the LUAD–LUSC axis. At the component level, AC and SCC components shared most driver mutations and exhibited broadly concordant CNV profiles, with clonal analysis supporting a monoclonal origin. Feature clustering highlighted the importance of 3q co-amplification, particularly in distinguishing component-level differences. Moreover, machine learning classifiers trained on LUAD and LUSC positioned ASC samples in the mid-spectrum through probabilistic soft scores. Collectively, these findings indicate that ASC represents a transitional molecular state between LUAD and LUSC, arising from a monoclonal origin and shaped by 3q amplification events. This study establishes a genomic framework for ASC, providing insights that may inform improved diagnostic strategies and future therapeutic development.

Keywords: Adenosquamous carcinoma, Monoclonal origin, Squamous transdifferentiation, Whole-exome sequencing, Copy-number variation, Machine learning

Introduction

Lung cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer worldwide, accounting for 12.4% of all cancer cases and 18.7% of cancer-related deaths, according to GLOBOCAN estimates [1]. In Taiwan, lung cancer ranks as the second most frequently diagnosed cancer, following female breast cancer, and remains the leading cause of cancer-related death [2]. Approximately 95% of these cases are classified as non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Among NSCLC subtypes, lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) and lung squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC) are predominant, representing 81.49% and 11.38% of invasive cases, respectively, based on the latest statistics reported by the Taiwan Cancer Registry [3].

While LUAD and LUSC are the main subtypes of NSCLC, adenosquamous carcinoma (ASC) is a rare but clinically significant subtype, noted for its higher aggressiveness and worse prognosis [4], accounting for 1.21% of NSCLC cases in Taiwan [3]. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) definition, ASC is histologically defined by the presence of both adenocarcinoma (AC) component and squamous cell carcinoma (SCC) component, with each comprising at least 10% of the tumor [5]. Due to its biphasic histology, ASC poses significant challenges in clinical diagnosis and treatment. Common diagnostic methods, such as biopsies, often provide only limited tissue samples. These small samples often capture only one histologic component, leading to frequent misdiagnosis by pathologists as either pure LUAD or LUSC. Notably, one study reported that only 2% of ASC cases were accurately diagnosed prior to surgery [6]. As a result, definitive diagnosis of ASC almost relies on thorough pathological evaluation of surgically resected specimens. Despite these histological criteria, the molecular mechanisms underlying ASC development remain poorly understood, highlighting the need for further investigation.

Among the various theories proposed to explain the histogenesis of ASC, the monoclonal origin hypothesis has gained the most support [7, 8]. This hypothesis proposes that the AC component and SCC component in ASC are not the result of a naive collision of two independent tumors, but instead originate from a single progenitor cell that has undergone malignant transformation. During subsequent proliferation and tumor evolution, this progenitor cell experiences divergent differentiation, giving rise to two distinct histological phenotypes [8]. The strongest evidence supporting this hypothesis comes from genomic studies. Researchers used laser capture microdissection to precisely isolate the AC component and the SCC component from the same ASC tumor specimen, subsequently performing next-generation sequencing (NGS) on each component separately [7-9]. These studies consistently found that although the two components are morphologically distinct, they share the exact same "trunk mutations." These trunk mutations are present in all cells of the tumor and typically occur in key driver genes, such as EGFR and TP53, during the early stages of cancer development [8]. This shared mutational profile serves as decisive evidence that they originate from a single progenitor cell.

The cancer stem cell model, in particular, offers a cell-level mechanistic explanation for this monoclonal origin. According to this model, ASC may arise from a single cancer stem cell with multipotent differentiation potential. This cancer stem cell is capable of both self-renewal to sustain tumor growth and differentiation into two distinct progenies: adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma cells [10]. Direct functional evidence supports this theory. In one study, researchers successfully isolated and cultured cancer stem-like cells from human ASC tumors. Remarkably, when a single cancer stem-like cell was implanted into an immunodeficient mouse, it independently regenerated a complete ASC tumor comprising both AC components and SCC components. This experiment provided compelling evidence that a single cell is sufficient to recapitulate the tumor heterogeneity [11]. Further analysis revealed that these cancer stem-like cells co-express lineage markers for both AC component (e.g., CK7) and SCC component (e.g., CK5) [11], thereby confirming their multipotent stem cell properties.

The adeno-to-squamous transition (AST) hypothesis provides a dynamic perspective that complements the monoclonal origin model, suggesting that the biphasic phenotype of ASC emerges through a progressive transdifferentiation process from adenocarcinoma to squamous cell carcinoma [12]. Clinical evidence of AST is supported by cases where EGFR-mutant LUAD transforms into LUSC following resistance to EGFR-tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) therapy [13]. Retention of the original EGFR mutations suggests that the squamous transformation occurs within the same tumor lineage. Similar transitions have also been observed following ALK inhibitor therapy [14, 15], immunotherapy [16], and chemotherapy [17], implying that AST may represent a broader adaptive response under therapeutic pressure. Further molecular and spatial transcriptomic evidence has reinforced this hypothesis. Zhao et al. performed whole-exome sequencing (WES) on multiple tumor components and metastatic samples from several ASC patients and found that both adenocarcinoma and squamous components commonly shared trunk mutations in key driver genes such as EGFR and MET, supporting a monoclonal origin [18]. By constructing phylogenetic trees, they proposed an evolutionary model in which ASC represents an intermediate state between LUAD and LUSC, capable of metastasizing into histologically pure subtypes at different stages. In addition, integration of Stereo-seq spatial transcriptomics and pseudo-time analysis revealed a transcriptional trajectory from adenocarcinoma to squamous differentiation within a single ASC tumor, suggesting a continuous phenotypic spectrum. The AST process was also validated in mouse xenograft models, where tumors across serial passages exhibited a gradual shift in histologic phenotype, characterized by increased expression of squamous markers such as p40 and decreased expression of adenocarcinoma markers like TTF-1.

Further evidence was provided by Qin et al., who established a transgenic mouse model expressing EML4-ALK fusion and demonstrated a time-dependent AST. The transition was shown to originate primarily from club cells rather than alveolar type II (AT2) cells, and was driven by phase separation of EML4-ALK, leading to activation of the JAK–STAT signaling pathway [19]. Organoid models and single-cell transcriptomic analyses revealed the presence of transitional cell populations co-expressing adenocarcinoma and squamous markers, highlighting a phenotypically plastic intermediate state. Notably, ALK-rearranged tumors exhibiting squamous features showed resistance to ALK inhibitors, which could be partially reversed by co-treatment with JAK1/2 inhibitors.

While the AST model has gained increasing support, comprehensive genomic studies of ASC remain scarce. Although many recent studies have incorporated WES or WGS data, these are often secondary to transcriptomic or histopathologic analyses, with limited focus on driver mutation landscape or copy number variations. Vassella et al. applied miR-205, a squamous-upregulated transcriptomic marker [20], to ASC and observed intermediate expression levels, lending support to the spectrum hypothesis [9]. However, their analysis was restricted to a single marker and did not address genome-wide alterations. Notably, known oncogenic drivers such as EML4-ALK fusions occur only in a small fraction of ASC cases [19], offering limited explanatory power for the disease as a whole. To bridge these gaps, we performed whole-exome sequencing on a Taiwanese ASC cohort and conducted integrative comparisons with LUAD and LUSC samples from East Asia. We further analyzed matched AC and SCC components to evaluate their clonal composition and molecular divergence. We also developed a soft score framework to position ASC samples along the LUAD–LUSC spectrum, offering potential insights for diagnosis and treatment.

Materials and Methods

Study overview

This study aimed to characterize the genomic features of ASC and compare them with pure LUAD and pure LUSC. We analyzed whole-exome sequencing (WES) data from 29 ASC patients from Taipei Veterans General Hospital (TVGH), with paired mixed tumor (T) and adjacent normal (N) tissues for all cases. In a subset of 11 patients, additional adenocarcinoma (A) and squamous (S) components were microdissected to investigate intra-tumor heterogeneity. For subtype-level comparisons, we further incorporated publicly available WES datasets from Taiwanese LUAD (n = 89) [21] and Korean LUSC (n = 82) [22] cohorts. Based on these resources, we designed two analytical frameworks: (i) a subtype-level comparison involving the 29 ASC T samples together with the external LUAD and LUSC cohorts, and (ii) a component-level comparison based on the paired A, S, and T samples from 11 ASC patients (Figure 1).

Figure 1. Overview of sample composition and cohort structure.

Salmon-colored circles indicate adenocarcinoma-like (A) components, steel blue circles indicate squamous-like (S) components, and sea green regions denote mixed tumor (T) components. LUAD and LUSC samples are shown as orange and blue blocks, respectively. These color assignments are used consistently throughout this study.

Our study conducted multi-level genomic analyses based on WES data. The overall workflow is summarized in Figure 2 and includes raw read quality control, alignment and BAM processing, detection of somatic single-nucleotide variants (SNVs) and insertions/deletions (INDELs), copy number variation (CNV) profiling and clustering, and variant annotation. These processed data were then used for downstream analyses, including clonal architecture inference, homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) assessment, microsatellite instability (MSI) evaluation, and extraction of COSMIC mutational signatures. We subsequently compared these genomic features at both the subtype-level and the component-level, aiming to delineate inter-subtype differences and to assess intra-tumor heterogeneity. Furthermore, we applied integrative approaches such as similarity analysis and dimensionality reduction to explore relationships among subtypes and components and developed machine learning models to extract discriminative features and to evaluate whether ASC represents an intermediate state between LUAD and LUSC.

Sample collection and sequencing

A total of 80 WES samples were collected from 29 patients with lung ASC at TVGH. Genomic DNA was isolated from sectioned formalin-fixed paraffin-embedded (FFPE) blocks using FFPE-compatible extraction reagents. DNA concentration and integrity were assessed by NanoDrop 2000 spectrophotometry and fragment analysis. Whole-exome libraries were generated with the Agilent SureSelect Human All-Exome V8 Kit and subjected to paired-end sequencing on an Illumina platform, producing >30 GB of tumor data and >7 GB of normal data per sample. Raw sequencing reads were obtained as FASTQ files, which served as the input for the downstream preprocessing and variant-calling pipeline.

The LUAD cohort was obtained from a publicly available dataset published by Academia Sinica, Taiwan (dbGaP accession: phs001954.v1.p1) [21], and the LUSC cohort was retrieved from a publicly available dataset generated at Seoul National University Hospital, Korea (SRA accession: SRP114315) [22]. To ensure consistency in downstream analyses, both cohorts were reprocessed from raw FASTQ files using the same WES pipeline. Further, the LUSC cohort was refined to ensure analytical consistency. The original cohort comprised 101 samples, but we excluded 19 samples that the original publication classified as "subtype B" [22]. These samples were characterized by a low tumor mutation burden, a lack of CNVs, and were genomically indistinguishable from normal tissue. This filtering resulted in a final cohort of 82 LUSC samples for analysis.

Somatic mutation analysis

Read alignment and BAM file processing

Sequencing reads in FASTQ format were first examined using FastQC for basic quality metrics [23]. Adapter sequences and low-quality bases were trimmed using Trimmomatic [24]. The cleaned reads were then aligned to the GRCh38 human reference genome using BWA-MEM [25], and the resulting alignments were converted to BAM format with SAMtools [26]. Subsequent processing involved sorting, marking duplicates (using Picard [27]), and recalibrating base quality scores via GATK4 [28]. The final BAM quality was assessed using Qualimap to ensure adequate coverage and alignment accuracy [29] (Figure 2). These steps generated high-quality BAM files suitable for downstream variant calling and CNV analysis.

Somatic variant calling and annotation

Somatic mutations were identified through a multi-tool consensus approach, following the framework of TCGA MC3 pipeline [30]. For SNV detection, we applied Mutect2 [28], MuSE [31], and VarScan2 [32]; for INDELs, we included Pindel [33], Mutect2, and VarScan2. Variants were filtered to retain those with a variant allele frequency (VAF) ≥ 0.05, total read depth (DP) > 30, and alternate allele depth (AD) > 3. Only variants detected by at least two of the tools were retained to reduce false positives. The resulting variant calls were merged using custom Python scripts. The filtered variants were then annotated with the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) [34] and converted into Mutation Annotation Format (MAF) using vcf2maf [35] (Figure 2).

Mutation landscape

We used pymaftools, a self-developed Python package, to generate oncoplots to visualize the somatic mutation landscape across all samples. These plots highlight the most frequently mutated genes (typically mutation frequency > 5%), with genes ranked by overall frequency and samples organized by either histological subtype or tumor component. At the subtype level, we compared LUAD, LUSC, and ASC to identify differences in mutational patterns, including alterations specific to individual subtypes. At the component level, we analyzed shared and private mutations between paired components to assess the extent of intra-tumor heterogeneity and clonal divergence.

Detection of significantly mutated genes

Significantly mutated genes (SMGs) were identified using MutSigCV [36], which estimates background mutation rates from observed silent and nearby non-coding mutations, with accuracy improved by pooling genes with similar properties such as replication timing and expression level. Genes with a q-value < 0.1 were considered significant, consistent with the original study. Olfactory receptor genes were excluded due to their late replication timing, low RNA expression levels, and lack of oncogenic relevance, as previously described [36]. We generated lollipop plots using pymaftools to visualize the distribution of mutations within protein domains and identify potential functional hotspots.

Genomic metrics derived from somatic mutations

To further characterize the mutational landscape, we calculated several genomic metrics. Tumor mutational burden (TMB) was defined as the number of nonsynonymous somatic mutations per megabase (Mb) of coding region, including frameshift deletions, frameshift insertions, in-frame deletions, in-frame insertions, missense, nonsense, splice site, translation start site, and nonstop mutations [37]. TMB values were normalized by panel size (66 Mb for LUAD, 41.6 Mb for ASC, and 50 Mb for LUSC) to account for differences in capture regions. MSI was assessed using MSIsensor2 [38], which computes the MSI score as the proportion of unstable microsatellite sites among all valid sites. A score greater than 20 was used to define MSI-high status, consistent with the cutoff applied in the TCGA pipeline. The transition/transversion (Ti/Tv) ratio was computed using pymaftools to further describe the mutational spectrum, where transitions represent purine-to-purine or pyrimidine-to-pyrimidine substitutions and transversions represent purine-to-pyrimidine substitutions. Finally, mutational signatures were extracted using SigProfilerMatrixGenerator [39] to generate trinucleotide context matrices (96 × n samples). De novo signature extraction was performed using SigProfilerExtractor [40], which inferred the optimal number of signatures by non-negative matrix factorization (NMF). Each signature was then compared with COSMIC v3 references using cosine similarity, and the most similar SBS signature was assigned.

Copy number variation analysis

Our CNV analysis involved two parallel bioinformatics pipelines to characterize different aspects of copy number variation. The first pipeline focused on identifying significant broad CNV events across the cohort, while the second focused on allele-specific copy number states and calculating HRD scores.

CNV estimation and recurrent region detection

CNVs were estimated from tumor BAM files using CNVkit [41] with matched normal samples as references. Segmented log2 copy ratios were analyzed with GISTIC2.0 [42] to identify significantly recurrent amplifications and deletions. GISTIC2.0 was run separately for the ASC, LUAD, and LUSC cohorts, and the resulting gene- and arm-level CNV profiles were combined for downstream comparative analyses across subtypes and components (Figure 2).

Allele-CNV and HRD analysis

For analyses requiring allele-specific resolution, such as HRD scoring and clonal inference, we implemented a multi-step analysis pipeline integrating Sequenza [43], scarHRD [44], and PyClone-VI [45] (Supplementary Figure 1). We first utilized Sequenza-utils to convert tumor BAM files into seqz files, which encode both B-allele frequency (BAF) and depth ratio information across the genome. Sequenza then estimated tumor cellularity (purity) and ploidy via grid search and inferred allele-specific copy number states (major and minor copy numbers) for each genomic segment. Following CNV estimation by Sequenza, HRD scores were calculated using the scarHRD algorithm [44]. scarHRD processes the .seqz files to re-estimate purity and ploidy, extract CNV profiles, and quantify three genome-wide measures, loss of heterozygosity (LOH), telomeric allelic imbalance (TAI), and large-scale state transitions (LST), which are summed to generate the final HRD score.

CNV analysis and CNV-based clustering

Gene-level and arm-level log2 copy number ratio tables were obtained from the GISTIC2.0 output. We first examined the overall CNV distribution using heatmaps to enable visual comparison across histological subtypes and components. These exploratory analyses revealed recurrent CNV gains and losses, which served as the basis for subsequent analyses.

Because gene-level CNV data are highly redundant, with neighboring genes within the same segment often sharing identical log2 ratio values, we implemented a feature integration strategy based on gene clustering to achieve a resolution between the arm and gene levels. Specifically, pairwise cosine distances between genes were computed to construct a gene–gene distance matrix, and agglomerative hierarchical clustering (average linkage) was applied to group genes into clusters (CNV blocks). For each sample, the mean CNV value of all genes within a block was used as the representative feature.

To determine the optimal number of clusters (k), we tested k values ranging from 2 to 200 using a cross-subsampling strategy to avoid overfitting. In each iteration, all samples were randomly divided into five disjoint subsets, and clustering was repeated five times using 80% of the data. For each k, clustering quality was evaluated using the silhouette score, which measures the compactness and separation of clusters (range −1 to 1, with higher values indicating better structure), as implemented in scikit-learn. The average silhouette score across all genes and folds was used to select the optimal cluster number. For these clusters, boxplots were generated to compare CNV values across tumor components and subtypes, with statistical tests applied to assess group differences. In addition, chromosome-wise line plots and heatmaps were used to visualize detailed CNV profiles of clusters of interest.

pymaftools framework

To enable consistent integration and downstream analysis of multi-omics features in this study, we developed a Python package, pymaftools, which provides a flexible and extensible framework for processing MAF files and related genomic data (Figure 3). The framework was used to align and organize SNV/INDEL, CNV, and mutational signature data across all samples, thereby facilitating comparative analyses and visualization of genomic features at both the component and subtype levels.

Figure 3. pymaftools overview.

PivotTable: unified matrix representation

The PivotTable class is designed to represent biological data in a matrix format of number of features by number of samples. It inherits from pandas.DataFrame class [46], while incorporating two additional metadata attributes: feature_metadata, which annotates the features (e.g., genes or clusters), and sample_metadata, which annotates the samples. These attributes are automatically aligned with the matrix index and columns, respectively. This structure enables more intuitive and flexible operations such as filtering, subsetting, ordering, and visualization of mutation data across different sample groups or genomic features.

Cohort: multi-modal data integration

The Cohort class provides an integrated interface for managing multiple PivotTable instances (e.g., SNV/INDEL, CNV, mutational signatures). By aligning and merging their corresponding sample_metadata, the Cohort object allows synchronized operations across different data modalities. This design supports consistent subsetting, ordering, and analysis at the cohort level, enabling integrated interpretation of multi-omics features.

To facilitate data persistence, sharing, and reproducibility, the Cohort class includes a save method that serializes the entire object, including all tables and metadata, into an SQLite database. This format ensures efficient storage, compatibility, and ease of restoration for downstream analysis.

Statistical testing

Statistical comparisons were conducted mainly using standard functions from the scipy.stats module [47]. For continuous variables in unpaired settings, two-group comparisons used the Mann–Whitney U test and multi-group comparisons used the Kruskal–Wallis test. For component-level analyses (A, T, S) in which measurements are paired within the same case, paired multi-group comparisons used the Friedman test, and paired two-group comparisons used the Wilcoxon signed-rank test. Categorical variables were assessed using the chi-squared test (scipy.stats.chi2_contingency). All adjusted p-values for multiple testing were calculated using the Benjamini–Hochberg (BH) procedure, provided in the statsmodels.stats.multitest module, to control the false discovery rate (FDR). For visualization, statistical annotations were added using the statannotations package, with significance levels denoted as ns (p > 0.05), * (p < 0.05), ** (p < 0.01), *** (p < 0.001), and **** (p < 0.0001).

Clonal analysis

For the clonal structure analysis of ASC components, we applied PyClone-VI, a Bayesian clustering method that infers cancer cell fractions (CCFs) of somatic mutations [45]. The analysis was performed on 33 tumor components from 11 ASC patients. To generate PyClone-compatible input, we integrated SNV data and their corresponding reference and alternate allele depths from the MAF files with allele-specific copy number information (major copy number, minor copy number, and total copy number of the segment in matched normal tissue) and estimated cellularity values from Sequenza output. The resulting tables enabled PyClone-VI to model the CCF distributions across tumor components, revealing insights into the clonal composition and evolutionary trajectory of ASC tumors. For each patient, we compared the distribution of CCFs and cluster assignments across the three components. By analyzing the clonal composition, we aimed to determine whether the components originated from a common ancestor clone (supporting the monoclonal hypothesis) or whether component-specific subclones were present, suggesting localized differentiation or polyclonal origins.

Similarity analysis

We quantified molecular similarity among samples to evaluate subtype separation. Pairwise similarity matrices were constructed using CNV (continuous table) and SNV/INDEL (binary table). We applied three similarity measures selected according to the type of genomic feature data. For continuous CNV values, we used cosine similarity, which measures the angle between two feature vectors and captures relative variation patterns regardless of magnitude. For binary SNV/INDEL profiles, we used the simple matching coefficient (SMC), which accounts for both the co-occurrence and co-absence of mutations, and the Jaccard similarity, which measures only the proportion of shared mutations while ignoring shared absence. To reduce the bias that may be caused by the sparse nature of the SNV/INDEL data, we restricted the analysis to genes mutated in at least 5% of samples. Since including all genes would make the data too sparse and dominated by unmutated sites, which would inflate similarity values and reduce biological interpretability. For each metric, we calculated the mean similarity within each subtype by averaging the similarity values across all possible sample pairs from the same subtype (e.g., all LUAD–LUAD pairs). Between-subtype similarity was calculated in the same way by averaging over all pairs formed by samples from two different subtypes (e.g., LUAD–ASC, LUAD–LUSC), resulting in six group-level similarities in the three-subtype setting. Statistical significance was assessed using a permutation test, in which sample subtype labels were randomly shuffled 1,000 times and the six similarities were recalculated in each iteration to generate an empirical null distribution. The p-value was defined as the proportion of permuted similarities exceeding the observed value, indicating whether the group similarity was significantly higher than expected by chance. We also applied the Mann–Whitney U test to directly compare the distributions of LUAD–ASC and ASC–LUSC similarities, thereby determining whether ASC is more similar to LUAD or to LUSC.

Dimensionality reduction

To visualize the distribution of samples and assess relationships between different genomic data modalities, we applied two dimensionality reduction methods: principal component analysis (PCA) and canonical correlation analysis (CCA). PCA reduces high-dimensional mutation and CNV matrices into low-dimensional projections for intuitive visualization of subtype distribution, while CCA evaluates the statistical correlations between modalities to assess their informational complementarity and integrative potential.

PCA was performed on both SNV/INDEL and CNV data, projecting high-dimensional matrices into two dimensions for visualization of inter-sample relationships. This analysis was used to examine the overall distribution of lung cancer subtypes as well as the relative positioning of different ASC components, thereby highlighting clustering patterns and potential transitional trajectories. CCA was employed to examine potential covariation structures between SNV/INDEL and CNV data. Prior to analysis, CNV features were normalized to a 0–1 range to reduce scale differences, while SNV/INDEL features were filtered to retain genes mutated in at least 5% of samples, since PCA is sensitive to data sparsity and low-variance features; using all genes would yield a near-empty mutation matrix dominated by zeros, weakening the covariance structure and obscuring subtype-specific patterns. To mitigate the high-dimensionality issue, both SNV/INDEL and CNV-cluster matrices were separately reduced to 20 principal components using PCA. CCA was then performed on these 20-dimensional representations to identify maximally correlated linear combinations between the two modalities. The first canonical variates from SNV/INDEL and CNV were extracted to quantify shared variation, and their projections were visualized using a scatter plot.

Model construction, evaluation, and application

To systematically evaluate the discriminative power of genomic features, we developed a two-stage model comparison strategy (Figure 4). In the first stage, classifiers were trained separately on SNV/INDELs mutated in at least 5% of samples and CNV features at multiple resolutions (CNV-gene, CNV-arm, and CNV-cluster levels) and compared with an integrative stacking model that combined SNV/INDEL and CNV inputs. In the second stage, we applied feature-reduction strategies to assess whether simplified subsets could retain predictive performance while improving interpretability. These include Recursive Feature Elimination with Cross-Validation (RFECV) and restricting inputs to SNV/INDELs mutated in at least 5% of samples and CNV clusters. All training procedures were performed on LUAD and LUSC samples only, with ASC cases excluded to avoid bias in subtype discrimination.

Figure 4. Model training overview.

SNV/INDEL features were encoded as binary variables, whereas CNV features were represented as log2 copy-number ratios, with cluster-level values computed as mean log2 ratios. For single-modality models, random forests with 100 estimators were used. For integrative modeling, random forests trained separately on SNV/INDEL and CNV features served as base learners, and their predicted probabilities were combined through a logistic regression meta-learner (implemented with scikit-learn StackingClassifier).

Two major axes of comparison were considered. First, classifiers trained on individual feature sets (SNV/INDEL, gene-level CNV, arm-level CNV, cluster-level CNV) were compared with the integrated stacking model. Second, combinations of SNV and CNV feature subsets were evaluated. For SNVs, two subsets were tested: genes mutated in at least 5% of samples (SNV_TOP), and RFECV-selected subsets of the top genes (SNV_RFE). For CNVs, two subsets were tested: all features (CNV_ALL) and RFECV-selected subsets (CNV_RFE).

Model robustness was ensured through repeated stratified cross-validation, with 5-fold splits repeated across 100 random seeds (yielding 500 train–test partitions). For each seed, model initialization and data partitioning were fixed, and models were freshly trained and evaluated on each split. Feature-importance values were recorded from random forest classifiers in each fold and aggregated across seeds to assess the stability of individual feature contributions. Performance was evaluated using accuracy, F1 score, and AUROC, which were calculated for each fold and summarized across all seeds. RFECV was applied to candidate feature sets (SNV/INDEL mutated in at least 5% of samples and CNV-cluster features) by iteratively eliminating features until an optimal subset was identified, defined as the point of maximal cross-validated accuracy while minimizing overfitting.

Finally, a stacking model was retrained using the optimal parameters. To position each sample along the LUAD–LUSC continuum, the predicted probability of the LUSC class was used as a continuous soft score ranging from 0 to 1, with higher values indicating greater similarity to LUSC and lower values indicating the sample is more similar to LUAD. Samples with intermediate scores were interpreted as having transitional or mixed profiles, thereby providing a quantitative framework to situate ASC cases along the LUAD–LUSC molecular spectrum.

Results

Subtype-level analysis

Mutation profile of subtypes

To compare the mutational landscape among lung cancer subtypes, we analyzed WES data from three cohorts: LUAD (n = 89), ASC (n = 29), and LUSC (n = 82). All samples were processed using the same variant calling pipeline to ensure consistency. Oncoplot visualization showed distinct mutation patterns across subtypes (Figure 5). EGFR mutations were most frequent in LUAD, followed by ASC, and least common in LUSC. In contrast, TP53 mutations exhibited the opposite trend, with the highest frequency observed in LUSC, followed by ASC, and the lowest in LUAD. The oncoplot indicates that LUSC harbors a markedly higher overall number of mutations than ASC and LUAD.

Figure 5. Oncoplot of SNV/INDEL mutation for three lung cancer subtypes.

This oncoplot illustrates the distribution of nonsynonymous SNV/INDEL mutations across tumor samples. The X-axis represents individual samples, annotated by subtype (LUAD, ASC, LUSC), sex, and smoking status. The Y-axis lists genes with nonsynonymous mutations observed in more than 10% of all samples. Each colored block indicates the presence of a mutation in the corresponding gene-sample pair, with colors representing mutation types (e.g., missense, nonsense, frameshift, etc.).

To identify significantly mutated genes (SMGs), we applied MutSigCV (q < 0.1). The analysis yielded TP53, EGFR, RBM10, and OR10C1 as SMGs in LUAD; EGFR and TP53 in ASC; and TP53 and OR14I1 in LUSC. Notably, OR10C1 and OR14I1 are olfactory receptor (OR) genes, which are typically lowly expressed and late-replicating—features associated with elevated background mutation rates. Therefore, OR genes were excluded from downstream analyses. We also found that RBM10 and TP53 exhibited a potential pattern of mutual exclusivity, as observed in the oncoplot (chi-square test, p=1.24×10⁻⁴, odds ratio=0.0813), warranting further investigation. These SMGs were then used to generate protein-level lollipop plots. The lollipop plots revealed that EGFR alterations in LUAD and ASC predominantly consisted of classical activating mutations in the tyrosine kinase domain, including exon 19 deletions and L858R substitutions. In contrast, EGFR mutations were virtually absent in LUSC (Figure 6a). TP53 mutations are widely distributed within the DNA-binding domain, rather than clustering around distinct hotspots. Notably, mutations in the tetramerization domain of TP53 were uniquely observed in LUSC, suggesting potential implications for p53 structural stability and function in this subtype (Figure 6b). Mutations in RBM10 were mostly scattered outside annotated functional domains, with only a few occurring within the RNA recognition motif domain, indicating that the pathogenic relevance of RBM10 alterations remains to be clarified (Figure 6c).

Although MSI-high is rare in lung cancer, previous research has linked MSI-high tumors to favorable responses to immune checkpoint inhibitors (ICIs), particularly anti-PD-1/PD-L1 therapies. Therefore, MSI analysis was included to evaluate the potential presence of mismatch repair deficiency and its relevance to immune response in different lung cancer subtypes, including ASC. As shown in Figure 7a, MSI scores were higher in LUSC and ASC compared to LUAD, but all samples were microsatellite-stable. To assess the overall mutation burden, which indirectly reflects the number of potential neoantigens and has been associated with ICI response, we compared TMB across subtypes. TMB showed a clear trend of LUSC > ASC > LUAD, with significant differences between all groups, suggesting that LUSC and ASC harbor more somatic mutations (Figure 7b). Because the Ti/Tv ratio serves as an indicator of mutational spectrum and is sensitive to tobacco-associated mutagenesis, we next examined Ti/Tv ratios. The Ti/Tv ratio was significantly lower in LUSC, meaning there were more transversion mutations, which are commonly caused by smoking. By contrast, ASC and LUAD displayed higher and similar Ti/Tv ratios, indicating more stable mutational patterns (Figure 7c). Since homologous recombination deficiency (HRD) reflects impaired DNA repair and genomic instability and has been associated with sensitivity to PARP inhibitors [48], we further compared HRD scores. LUSC exhibited the highest HRD scores among the subtypes, suggesting a greater degree of structural alterations and repair defects. ASC and LUAD showed relatively lower HRD scores (Figure 7d).

To investigate mutational processes underlying tumor development, we analyzed COSMIC mutational signatures. LUSC was strongly enriched for SBS4, the signature associated with tobacco-induced DNA damage (Figure 8), consistent with its lower Ti/Tv ratio and smoking-related mutation spectrum. In contrast, the age-related signature SBS1 was significantly less prevalent in LUSC compared to ASC and LUAD, while another age-related signature, SBS5, was more prominent in LUAD relative to the other two subtypes (Figure 8). Finally, SBS6, linked to mismatch repair deficiency, contributed more substantially in ASC, suggesting a higher prevalence of repair-associated alterations in this subtype (Figure 8). These comparisons indicate that while ASC shares features with both LUAD and LUSC, it also harbors a distinctive mutational process profile.

CNV landscape and clustering in subtypes

In addition to examining the SNV/INDEL oncoplot and other genomic metrics, we also calculated CNVs from WES data at different resolutions for comparative analysis. At the gene level, the heatmap revealed widespread co-amplification and co-deletion among adjacent genes (Figure 10a). Using the Kruskal–Wallis test, we identified a large number of genes with significant differences among the three subtypes (q < 0.1, 17,566 significant out of 25,988 genes). In contrast, the arm-level heatmap provided a more concise representation (Figure 10b). Statistical testing showed that 31 chromosomal arms exhibited significant differences among the three subtypes (q < 0.1, Supplementary Table 1), with the top ten including 3q, 5q, 12p, 12q, 4p, 4q, 2p, 1p, 21q, and 21p. Nevertheless, arm-level analysis lacks sufficient resolution to identify focal alterations. To balance resolution and interpretability, we performed hierarchical clustering on gene-level log2 CNV values, defining 114 CNV clusters based on the average silhouette score (Supplementary Figure 3). These clusters represent co-amplified or co-deleted genomic segments and allow more refined comparisons among subtypes. Statistical testing revealed that 78 of 114 clusters were significantly different (q < 0.1, Supplementary Table 2). Representative examples include Cluster 47 (3q, 817 genes) corresponding to a large-scale 3q amplification, Cluster 6 (5q, 1,008 genes) reflecting a broad 5q deletion, and Cluster 35 (4q, 2 genes) capturing a focal alteration within 4q. Notably, there was substantial concordance between arm-level and cluster-level results. Arm-level analysis identified 31 significant chromosomal arms, while cluster-level analysis revealed 78 significant CNV clusters distributed across 36 arms. Among these, 29 arms were shared between the two approaches (Figure 11), indicating that most arm-level signals were also captured by the finer cluster-level analysis. The additional arms identified only at the cluster level likely reflect focal alterations that do not reach significance when averaged over the entire arm. These results demonstrate that the cluster-based approach recapitulates most arm-level signals while providing finer resolution to distinguish focal from broad events.

Similarity analysis across subtypes

To further compare genomic relationships among subtypes, we assessed SNV/INDEL similarity (restricted to genes mutated in at least 5% of samples) and CNV similarity. For SNV/INDEL, we quantified similarity using Jaccard (considering only shared mutations) and SMC (overall concordance including both shared mutations and shared absence). Although the absolute mean Jaccard values were low across all groups, permutation tests confirmed that LUAD and LUSC exhibited significantly higher within-group similarity than expected by chance, consistent with the presence of recurrent subtype-specific mutations. In contrast, ASC showed lower within-group Jaccard similarity, reflecting a more heterogeneous mutational landscape (Figure 12a). Under the SMC metric, however, ASC displayed the highest and statistically significant within-group similarity, suggesting that despite fewer consistent shared mutations, ASC samples share a largely wild-type background across most genes (Figure 12b). Both Jaccard and SMC mean similarity values of SNV/INDEL indicated that ASC was significantly similar to LUAD than to LUSC (Supplementary Figure 4a).

For CNVs, LUSC consistently exhibited the highest mean within-group similarity across all resolutions, whereas ASC and LUAD showed lower similarity. Furthermore, within-group similarity was significant for LUAD and LUSC across all three resolutions, whereas ASC reached significance only at the cluster-level and gene-level (Figure 13). Both gene-level and arm-level CNV similarity analyses indicated that ASC was significantly similar to LUAD than to LUSC, whereas no significant difference was observed at the cluster level (Supplementary Figure 4b). We interpret the lack of significance as a cluster-level artifact: some clusters capture focal CNV events, and when these focal regions are dissimilar across subtypes, they dilute the overall similarity because clusters are effectively equally weighted with broad/arm-level events. This equal weighting can attenuate subtype-specific signals, making ASC appear similarly related to both LUAD and LUSC at this resolution.

Component-level analysis

Mutation profile of components

In addition to comparing ASC with other subtypes, we analyzed component samples from 11 patients to investigate intra-tumor heterogeneity. Oncoplot visualization revealed that different components from the same patient generally exhibited similar mutation patterns, with key driver genes such as EGFR and TP53 frequently shared across components (Figure 16a). No significant differences were observed among mutational signatures (Figure 16b) or in genomic metrics, including MSI score, TMB, Ti/Tv ratio, and HRD score (Figure 16c), across components. We further examined mutations in SMGs identified in the previous section, including EGFR, RBM10, and TP53. In most cases, SMG mutations were shared across the three components (Figure 17). Here, we defined shared mutations as variants present across all three components (A, T, and S) of the same case, whereas private mutations were determined by comparing the pure AC and SCC components, with mixed tumor samples excluded to avoid confounding by mixed signals. Specifically, A-private mutations were those found in AC but not in SCC, and vice versa for S-private mutations. Importantly, shared or private status was determined at the variant level, requiring the same genomic position and nucleotide/amino acid change across components. We further assessed recurrent events, defining recurrent shared and recurrent private mutations as those detected in at least two independent cases. Among recurrent shared events, we found that five cases harbored an EGFR recurrent shared missense mutation. Further examination of the amino acid change revealed that all these corresponded to the canonical L858R mutation (Figure 18a). For recurrent private nonsynonymous mutations, two cases showed an RPS3A missense mutation was an A-private (Figure 18b), and two cases carried an HLA-DQB1 missense mutation, which was an S-private (Figure 18c). Other recurrent private mutations were all synonymous. To further investigate clonal relationships, we applied PyClone clustering. In most ASC cases, the AC, SCC, and mixed components shared the same major clone, defined as the cluster with the highest cancer cell fraction. This result supports a common clonal origin of ASC despite the histological diversity of its components (Figure 19).

Model training

In the preceding genomic analyses, we compared the mutation profiles, CNV landscapes, and overall similarities across the three subtypes, and we further observed a continuous spectrum between LUAD and LUSC in PCA, while CCA further revealed significant covariation between SNV/INDEL and CNV. These findings suggested that integrating the two modalities could more comprehensively capture subtype differences. Based on this rationale, we sought to build classification models with two objectives: first, to identify the most discriminative features across modalities; and second, to use probabilistic model outputs to position ASC samples along the LUAD–ASC–LUSC continuum. To this end, we designed a two-stage modeling framework, excluding all ASC samples from the training process.

The results showed that the SNV/INDEL model performed relatively poorly, whereas among the CNV-based models, the CNV-cluster model consistently achieved the highest accuracy and stability, outperforming both CNV-gene and CNV-arm (Table 1, Figure 25a). This indicates that cluster-level CNV features capture stronger discriminative signals compared to other resolutions. Further, the stacking model outperformed all single-modality models on average (Table 1, Figure 25a), demonstrating that cross-modality integration improved performance. Since the CNV-cluster model was the top performer among the CNV-based models, we selected the CNV-cluster model as the representative CNV modality for the second-stage analysis.

In the second stage, we further assessed the impact of feature selection on the stacking model. We applied recursive feature elimination with cross-validation (RFECV) separately to SNV/INDEL features (frequency > 0.05) and CNV-cluster features, selecting 70 SNV/INDEL and 44 CNV-cluster features (Supplementary Figure 6), and trained models with different feature subset combinations. The results showed that even with reduced feature numbers, model performance remained stable. Among the six stacking configurations, the SNV-RFE–CNV-RFE combination performed best, achieving the highest mean values across all evaluation metrics and the lowest mean standard deviations (Table 2, Figure 25b). This configuration combined high accuracy with high stability and was therefore chosen as the configuration for final model training.

After identifying SNV-RFE–CNV-RFE as the best-performing configuration, we retrained this model on 80% of the LUAD and LUSC data (with a fixed random seed of 42) and evaluated it on the held-out 20%, achieving an accuracy of 0.962. Overall, CNV features contributed slightly more to the final model (weight = 0.5321) compared to SNV/INDEL features (weight = 0.4679). While CNVs dominated classification performance, SNV/INDELs, though weaker as a standalone modality, provided complementary driver mutation signals that fine-tuned the stacking model. We then used the predicted probability of each sample being classified as LUSC as a soft score, which was then applied to ASC samples. The resulting scores placed ASC cases between LUAD and LUSC, with a slight leaning toward LUAD (Figure 28a). A similar pattern was observed when examining ASC component samples, although the differences did not reach statistical significance (Figure 28b). To further explore subtype relationships in an unsupervised setting, we applied PCA to reduce the SNV-RFE and CNV-RFE feature matrices of LUAD and LUSC samples to 20 dimensions each, followed by CCA to capture the shared structure between the two modalities. All samples, including ASC, were then projected into the canonical space, where annotation by soft scores revealed a smooth gradient along the LUAD–LUSC axis (Figure 28c), whereas annotation by true subtypes showed the expected separation between LUAD and LUSC with ASC distributed in between (Figure 28d). Taken together, these results support the notion that ASC occupies an intermediate position between LUAD and LUSC, which can be more sensitively captured by probability-based soft scores and cross-omics CCA projection.

Discussion and Conclusions

This study conducted a multi-level comparative analysis of WES data in ASC to systematically characterize its genomic features, including comparisons across subtypes and components, with the aim of elucidating its tumor origin and potential mechanisms underlying AST. To enable efficient processing and visualization of multi-modal data, we also introduced and applied the pymaftools package, which supports integrative analysis of SNV/INDEL and CNV profiles. With this framework, we first analyzed the mutational profile of ASC at the subtype level. We observed that ASC exhibits intermediate profiles across multiple genomic metrics (MSI, TMB, Ti/Tv ratio, HRD score), lying between those of LUAD and LUSC (Figure 7). Similarity analysis further revealed that, in subtype-level comparisons, the ASC–LUAD similarity scores were on average higher than the ASC–LUSC scores, indicating that ASC as a group aligns more closely with LUAD than with LUSC in this analysis (Supplementary Figure 4). At the level of driver gene alterations, the hotspot EGFR mutations commonly detected in ASC resembled those found in LUAD, supporting the notion that ASC shares a molecular background more similar to LUAD (Figure 6). The PCA analysis of both CNV and SNV/INDEL profiles consistently positioned ASC samples between LUAD and LUSC, reinforcing their intermediate placement along the LUAD–ASC–LUSC molecular continuum (Figure 14). The CCA analysis further supports the interpretation that SNV/INDEL and CNV alterations are not independent but covary in a coordinated manner (Figure 15), providing the rationale for integrating both modalities in a stacking model. Consistently, the soft score derived from this model positioned ASC samples between LUAD and LUSC along a continuous spectrum (Figure 28). This model provides a practical framework for subtype interpretation and heterogeneity assessment, and may inform future translational and mechanistic studies.

Subsequently, we performed analyses at the component level. The study of SNV/INDEL profiles and clonal architecture, as estimated by PyClone, revealed that the major clone (the cluster with the highest CCF) was simultaneously present in the AC component, SCC component, and mixed tumor samples of the vast majority of cases (Figure 19). Key driver mutations, such as those in EGFR and TP53, were highly shared across all three components, with very few private mutations observed (Figure 17, Figure 18). Furthermore, other genomic metrics (MSI, TMB, Ti/Tv ratio, HRD score) showed no significant differences among the components, indicating a high degree of molecular homogeneity within cases (Figure 16). These results strongly support a monoclonal origin, suggesting that ASC originates from a single ancestral cell that undergoes diverse differentiation, rather than being a naïve collision tumor.

At the mutational level, we observed that some ASC cases contained SMGs such as EGFR, TP53, or RBM10. These mutations may affect cell cycle regulation and DNA repair through different mechanisms. For example, EGFR activation can promote the PI3K/AKT pathway and accelerate cell proliferation, whereas loss-of-function mutations in TP53 or RBM10 may impair error surveillance and genomic maintenance. Notably, RBM10 mutations exhibited a near-mutual exclusivity with TP53 alterations (Figure 5), suggesting that RBM10 inactivation serves as an alternative mechanism to indirectly suppress p53 function [57] and compromise cell cycle control.

In the CNV analysis, we identified 114 clusters and focused on those with the highest discriminative power in the classification model. Among them, we further examined Cluster 47 and Cluster 6, which exhibited the greatest feature importance and displayed a stepwise distribution pattern across subtypes (Figure 26). Cluster 47 includes genes located along the entire long arm of chromosome 3 (3q). We observed that LUAD samples exhibited the lowest mean log2 CNV values, LUSC the highest, and ASC fell in between, forming a clear stepwise distribution across subtypes. At the component level, all SCC components showed significantly higher log2 CNV values than their paired AC components, suggesting that this module may represent a shared and biologically meaningful marker during AST (Figure 27). In contrast, another top-ranking module, Cluster 6 (chromosome 5q), showed an opposite trend. We noted a clear stepwise distribution for 5q CNV, where LUAD exhibited the highest log ratio, followed by ASC, and LUSC the lowest. However, at the component level, the SCC components initially showed a higher average CNV log ratio (Figure 27). Upon closer inspection of individual samples, we determined this was attributable to a single outlier. This suggests that, whereas 5q alterations showed discordant patterns between the subtype and component levels, 3q alterations were reproducible across both levels and thus warranted further gene-level investigation. To gain a more detailed view of the CNV distribution along 3q, we plotted gene-level line charts sorted by gene position on the chromosome. The results showed that LUSC samples exhibited consistently higher CNV values across the entire 3q region compared to LUAD and ASC, with nearly all genes surpassing the amplification threshold of 0.1 (Supplementary Figure 7a). In ASC, most genes exceeded this threshold, whereas LUAD displayed a largely neutral profile. Notably, amplification events in the distal region of 3q were more frequently observed in both LUSC and ASC samples. At the component level, the SCC component also showed relatively elevated CNV ratios across this region, further supporting its association with squamous transformation (Supplementary Figure 7b). This observation is consistent with previous reports, which identified the region at 3q26-28 in LUSC and other squamous cell carcinomas as a key genomic locus involved in squamous differentiation [50]. This region contains several key genes, including PIK3CA (3q26.32), SOX2 (3q26.33), and TP63 (3q28). To clarify their functional roles, we further investigated the biological significance of these three genes in the context of squamous differentiation.

Among these genes, TP63 is a member of the TP53 gene family. Its predominant isoform, ΔNp63, lacks transactivation capability, in contrast to the pro-apoptotic isoform TAp63. ΔNp63 plays a key role in cellular differentiation and squamous epithelial development [51]. It activates genes associated with squamous cell identity, such as KRT5, KRT14, and components of the Notch signaling pathway, and functions as a central regulator of squamous lineage specification. SOX2 is a transcription factor involved in the maintenance of stemness [52]. In addition to its high expression in embryonic stem cells, it is abundantly expressed in basal cells of the airway epithelium, where it contributes to epithelial cell differentiation and remodeling. In lung cancer, SOX2 and TP63 are frequently co-expressed and cooperatively maintain the squamous phenotype of LUSC by establishing a stable transcriptional program for squamous differentiation. Recent studies further revealed that TP63 and SOX2 cooperatively activate the long non-coding RNA CCAT1 through super-enhancer regulation. CCAT1, in turn, forms a complex with TP63 and SOX2 to regulate EGFR expression, thereby activating both the MEK/ERK and PI3K/AKT pathways, ultimately promoting LUSC tumorigenesis [53]. PIK3CA is a frequently amplified and mutated gene in squamous cell carcinoma, encodes a catalytic subunit of PI3K, which activates the PI3K/AKT/mTOR signaling pathway, thereby promoting cell proliferation, survival, and metabolism [54]. The activation of this pathway has been implicated in the growth and transcriptional reprogramming of various epithelial tumors and may also play a supportive role in squamous differentiation. In addition to genomic activation of PI3K signaling through PIK3CA amplification, transcriptional regulation by the TP63–SOX2–CCAT1 axis converges on EGFR–PI3K/AKT signaling, highlighting a dual mechanism reinforcing squamous differentiation and tumor progression [53].

As a result, we propose a hypothesis that the 3q amplification observed in the SCC component of ASC not only reflects the activation of the squamous fate program, but also a key genomic event that drives the AST process, driven by the interplay between cell fate reprogramming and oncogenic signaling amplification. Furthermore, by combining this with the upregulation of gene expression caused by CNV amplification, known as the gene dosage effect [55], we propose a mechanistic inference: the amplification of PIK3CA can activate the PI3K/AKT/mTOR pathway, which in turn leads to increased cellular replication stress and genomic instability. This prompts additional chromosomal amplification, forming a positive feedback loop. Within this dynamic process, SOX2 and TP63, which are closely associated with squamous differentiation, may also undergo co-amplification, leading cells to progressively exhibit squamous characteristics and further reinforcing the direction of transdifferentiation (Figure 29). Notably, previous studies have also indicated that inhibiting PI3K/AKT signaling can restore sensitivity to EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors (such as Osimertinib) in drug-resistant tumor cells with squamous features [56]. This further supports the critical role of this pathway in both squamous transformation and treatment response. Although further experimental validation is needed, the proposed mechanism may help elucidate the developmental process of ASC and provide a foundation for future investigations into the molecular mechanisms and potential therapeutic targets of transdifferentiated lung cancer.

Based on the mechanisms described above, our integrative analysis suggests that key SMGs in ASC, including TP53, EGFR, and RBM10, together with recurrent 3q amplification (harboring PIK3CA, TP63, and SOX2), converge on cell cycle regulation and apoptosis (Figure 30). On the p53 axis, TP53 loss-of-function directly impairs p53 activity, RBM10 inactivation destabilizes p53 through MDM2 interaction, and ΔNp63 competitively suppresses p53 function. On the PI3K axis, PIK3CA amplification establishes a positive feedback loop, while EGFR phosphorylation and the TP63–SOX2–CCAT1 complex further enhance downstream PI3K/AKT signaling. Collectively, these alterations create a state of cell cycle vulnerability, leading to replication stress, genomic instability, and increased susceptibility to additional CNV events.

Integrating the findings from our multi-level analysis with existing literature, we propose that the AST process is not driven by a single driver event, but rather results from transcriptional reprogramming induced by the interplay of multiple intrinsic and extrinsic factors [56]. We propose a multi-factor model to explain the mechanisms underlying the AST process. First, an elevated background mutation rate increases the probability of CNV events. This is mainly driven by chemical mutagens from smoking and the loss of tumor suppressor activity. In particular, the function of TP53 can be suppressed by RBM10 through MDM2-mediated destabilization and by ΔNp63 via DNA-binding domain competition, thereby further increasing the mutation rate. Second, cell cycle dysregulation exacerbates replication stress and genomic alterations. This is driven by regenerative stress induced by smoking or chronic injury, as well as hyperactivation of the PI3K pathway (including EGFR–PI3K signaling, PIK3CA amplification, and CCAT1 regulation). Third, selective pressure from targeted therapies (e.g., EGFR-TKIs) may also promote transcriptional reprogramming of tumor cells. Finally, beyond genomic alterations, the intrinsic differentiation potential of the cell of origin also shapes susceptibility to transdifferentiation. Mouse and single-cell studies have shown that club cells possess greater plasticity than alveolar type II (AT2) cells, predisposing them to develop tumors with squamous features [19]. Moreover, most LUSCs arise from basal cells, which can differentiate into nearly all pulmonary epithelial lineages. Together, these factors may induce transcriptional reprogramming, thereby converging to shape the LUAD–ASC–LUSC spectrum observed in our analyses.

Nonetheless, these interpretations should be viewed in light of several limitations. The ASC cohort size was relatively small, and the LUAD and LUSC samples, obtained from public datasets, exhibited imbalances in sex and smoking status, potentially introducing bias. Moreover, this study focused solely on WES-level data, without incorporating epigenomic, transcriptomic, or spatial information. Future studies should expand sample size and integrate single-cell DNA, spatial genomics, and other multi-omics data to further validate the proposed model and elucidate the mechanisms of transdifferentiation. Despite these limitations, our current findings already provide important insights. Through multi-level genomic analyses, we demonstrated that ASC exhibits an intermediate molecular profile between LUAD and LUSC across various genomic features, providing strong evidence supporting both a monoclonal origin and a transdifferentiation model. Building on these findings, we propose a multifactorial model of transdifferentiation, emphasizing the interplay between genomic instability, cellular differentiation potential, and external stress. These factors may collectively shape the intermediate molecular characteristics observed in ASC. While further spatial and temporal data are needed to validate our hypotheses, the integrated framework proposed here may help recontextualize the molecular classification of ASC and offer new directions for investigating its underlying mechanisms and therapeutic vulnerabilities.
